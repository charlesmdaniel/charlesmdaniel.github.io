39 Rebrands Later…

The Architecture of Identity: Turning Repeated Change into Structured Clarity

Your eyes are not deceiving you. 39 times. But those name changes, aesthetic struggles, countless brand equity repositions, and even a confused ambition pipeline weren’t for the sake of severe cognitive dissonance or low self-worth. I was shedding. Shedding incomplete versions of myself. Each rebrand was based on a genuine search for meaning, not vanity redeployment.

Like most artists I’ve had the pleasure of meeting and working with, I tried to separate church and state. Keep the art persona on X and IG, be the enterprising Finance major on LinkedIn that engages with posts now and then, and leverage Meta as a platform for family and achievement. The obvious fragmentation led to constant worry of being seen and judged by unlikely targets, which could deeply threaten my purposely crafted fragmentation. It wasn’t until recently that I realized that I can’t please everybody, and I must embody my truth.

The whole truth.

I subconsciously believed my God-given gifts were separate departments rather than expressions of one architecture. This added pressure on a fractured nucleus, given childhood trauma that we’ll save for another time. The rebrands failed not because my work was weak or inefficient. But because the foundation had not yet been named.

The pattern I recognized, which underpinned my sharp, transitory phases, was the desire to turn my raw vision into a coherent asset under a tyrannical model of control. Not only is it unfeasible, but it also leans toward perceptual engineering in a way that brutally betrays first principles. Not only that, but it also serves as an obvious contradiction to the whole “vanity redeployment” point I made earlier. The desire to control, the tyrannical model I subconsciously built for my projects, is rooted in an emotion that governs the lives of nearly every human:

Fear.

Once I overcame that fear and fulfilled my desire to share the limitations of fear through a candid stream of (since deleted) tweets, the name of my foundation became clear. I’m not just an Analyst. Or a finance major. Or rapper. Or music producer. Or an audio engineer. Or a graphic designer. Or author. Or film director. Or a tax preparer. Or pipeline builder. Or dependent operator: I am all of those and more. I am a Creative Systems Architect. Someone who designs and builds structured systems that turn creative vision, narrative, and ideas into tangible assets, opportunities, and outcomes.

The music? Systems-building. The website? Systems-building. The writing? Systems-building. The networking, positioning, and pipeline thinking were all systems-building. I was not changing myself:

I was obsessively circling the nucleus that was fractured when I was a child.

Pattern recognized. Pattern utilized. Pattern created. Being able to name the path that gives me meaning provides a clarity that warms my core. The principle is clear. I no longer do things at a whim or because I can. The title, Creative Systems Architect, is a frame through which art, AI, business, and narrative can coexist without contradiction.

Now that I have a name and a clear path, the real work begins. I am not done becoming; I have simply ceased misnaming who I am. That phase is officially over.

After many disguises, detours, and private reckonings, I did not become someone new. Instead, I finally identified who I was all along. A Creative Systems Architect ready to fulfill his God-given purpose:

To build cool things to help solve real problems.
